Women in STEM

Celebrating brilliant women who shaped science, technology, engineering, and math.

Ada Lovelace

1815 – 1852

Daughter of poet Lord Byron, Ada Lovelace combined imagination with mathematics to write the first algorithm for Charles Babbage’s Analytical Engine. She foresaw the potential for machines to go beyond calculations, making her the world’s first computer programmer and a visionary for modern computing.

Marie Curie

1867 – 1934

Marie Curie was a pioneering physicist and chemist who discovered the elements polonium and radium. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize and remains the only person to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences—Physics and Chemistry. Her work on radioactivity laid the foundation for cancer treatment and nuclear physics.

Janaki Ammal

1897 – 1984

Janaki Ammal was an Indian botanist and cytogeneticist known for her groundbreaking research in plant genetics and breeding. Her work helped improve crop resilience and agricultural diversity. She also contributed to botanical taxonomy, leaving a lasting legacy in both scientific research and environmental conservation.

Rosalind Franklin

1920 – 1958

Rosalind Franklin was an English chemist and X-ray crystallographer whose work was critical in discovering the double-helix structure of DNA. Her X-ray diffraction images provided the essential evidence used by Watson and Crick, revolutionizing molecular biology and genetics.

Vera Rubin

1928 – 2016

Vera Rubin was an American astronomer whose pioneering observations of galaxy rotation rates provided strong evidence for the existence of dark matter. Her research transformed our understanding of the universe and helped shape modern cosmology. Rubin was a strong advocate for women in science.

Chien-Shiung Wu

1912 – 1997

Chien-Shiung Wu, known as the "First Lady of Physics," was a Chinese-American experimental physicist who conducted groundbreaking experiments in nuclear physics. Her work on beta decay confirmed important theoretical predictions, challenging previously accepted laws of physics and paving the way for future discoveries.
